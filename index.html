A Reference, Not a Practice Notebook

How do we know it still works
once we're not in the room?

The frameworks academic public health, international development, and learning health systems use to ask whether something that worked once will keep working when scaled, handed off, or run by someone else.

Why This Notebook Looks Different From the Others
  • No AI practice mode. These fields value precision, citation, and peer-reviewed nuance over rehearsal — a fabricated scenario would do this discipline a disservice. This is a cheatsheet for recognising and correctly applying the right framework, not a sandbox for practicing it.
  • Built for a different reader. Consulting frameworks assume one person, fast, under deadline, persuading a client. These assume a multi-year program, evaluated by people who weren't there at the start, accountable to a funder who wasn't either.
  • They cluster late. Almost everything here lives at the Prove and Scale end of a project, not the Diagnose end — see the Journey map on the notebook hub for how this set slots in against the other three notebooks.
  • Acronym-dense on purpose. This vocabulary was refined to survive peer review, not a two-week training. Density here signals precision, not inaccessibility — each entry below unpacks the acronym plainly.
  • Start with CFIR if you're new to this field. It's the most comprehensive and most-cited determinant framework, and most other frameworks here either narrow it (i-PARIHS, NPT) or sit downstream of it (RE-AIM, Realist Evaluation). Read it first, then branch based on what you're actually trying to do — diagnose barriers (stay in Determinant), judge success (go to Evaluation), or move evidence into routine practice (go to Process).

Start Here
CFIR — Consolidated Framework for Implementation Research
A comprehensive, "meta-theoretical" taxonomy of everything that can help or hinder implementation, synthesised from 19 existing theories.
Five Domains
Innovation Outer Setting Inner Setting Individuals Implementation Process
What it's actually for

A diagnostic checklist of ~40 constructs (e.g. evidence strength, organisational culture, leadership engagement) used to identify barriers and facilitators before, during, or after implementation — formative, process, or outcome evaluation.

Use it when
  • You need a comprehensive scan of "what could go wrong" before rolling something out
  • Comparing why the same intervention succeeded at one site and failed at another
Caveat

Comprehensive to the point of overwhelming — most teams select a relevant subset of constructs rather than applying all ~40. Not a process model; it tells you what matters, not in what order to act.

i-PARIHS — Integrated PARIHS
Successful implementation = active facilitation of an innovation with its recipients, in their context. Facilitation is the "active ingredient" — not a passive backdrop.
Four Core Constructs
Innovation Recipients Context Facilitation
What it's actually for

Unlike CFIR's flat checklist, i-PARIHS centres on facilitation — the specific role and skillset of the person enabling change — as the mechanism that integrates the other three constructs. It distinguishes novice, experienced, and expert facilitator roles.

Use it when
  • The implementation depends heavily on a designated change agent or facilitator role
  • You want to plan facilitation strategy, not just catalogue barriers
Caveat

Originated in nursing/health services — adapting "facilitation" language to non-clinical settings takes some translation. Often used retrospectively as an organising device rather than prospectively to design facilitation, which limits its planning power.

Determinant Framework May & Finch, 2009
Normalization Process Theory (NPT)
Explains how a new practice becomes routine, taken-for-granted work — the mechanism by which something stops being "the new thing" and starts being "just how we do it."
Four Core Constructs
Coherence Cognitive Participation Collective Action Reflexive Monitoring
What it's actually for

Coherence asks if people understand and make sense of the new practice. Cognitive participation asks if they buy in and commit. Collective action asks if the work actually gets done in practice. Reflexive monitoring asks if people evaluate and adapt it over time. All four must function for normalization to occur.

Use it when
  • You're past initial adoption and trying to understand why something hasn't stuck
  • You need a theory of embedding, not just initial uptake
Caveat

Explanatory, not prescriptive — NPT tells you why normalization succeeded or failed, but doesn't hand you a ready-made strategy. Most useful paired with a process framework like i-PARIHS or KTA.

Evaluation Evaluation Frameworks — assessing whether and why it worked
Evaluation Framework Glasgow et al., 1999 · 20-year review 2019
RE-AIM
Five dimensions that together measure the real-world, population-level impact of a program — corrective to research that only ever reports whether the intervention worked in ideal trial conditions.
Five Dimensions
Reach Effectiveness Adoption Implementation Maintenance
What it's actually for

Reach and Effectiveness operate at the individual/participant level. Adoption and Implementation operate at the staff/setting/system level. Maintenance applies to both — sustained delivery and sustained effectiveness over time. A program can excel at Effectiveness in a trial and still fail at population impact if Reach or Adoption are weak.

Use it when
  • Reporting or designing a public health program meant to scale beyond a controlled pilot
  • You need to distinguish "did it work" from "did it actually reach and stick with real-world settings"
Caveat

In practice, Reach is the dimension most often reported weakly or not at all — the 20-year review found it shows the largest gap between stated importance and actual reporting. Iterative RE-AIM (repeated scoring over time) is increasingly preferred over a single static assessment.

Evaluation Framework Pawson & Tilley, 1997
Realist Evaluation (CMO Configuration)
Rejects "does it work?" as the wrong question. Asks instead: what works, for whom, in what circumstances, and why.
Core Logic
Context Mechanism Outcome
What it's actually for

A CMO statement reads: "in this context, this mechanism fired for these people, generating this outcome." The same intervention can produce different outcomes in different contexts because different underlying mechanisms are triggered — realist evaluation tests and refines these CMO configurations against empirical data rather than asking for a single yes/no verdict on effectiveness.

Use it when
  • The same program produced wildly different results across sites and you need to explain why, not just report the average
  • You suspect "what works" genuinely depends on who and where, not just whether the program existed
Caveat

"Mechanism" is notoriously inconsistently defined across realist studies — nearly half of published studies don't explicitly define it. Method-neutral by design (qualitative or quantitative data both fit), which means rigour depends heavily on the evaluator's discipline, not the framework itself.

Evaluation Framework OECD/DAC, early 2000s · revised 2019
OECD-DAC Evaluation Criteria
The standard six-lens framework for evaluating development and humanitarian interventions, used globally by donors and multilaterals.
Six Criteria
Relevance Coherence Effectiveness Efficiency Impact Sustainability
What it's actually for

Relevance and Coherence assess fit with needs and other actors' efforts. Effectiveness and Efficiency assess delivery. Impact and Sustainability assess longer-term and lasting change. Coherence was added in the 2019 revision specifically to capture how an intervention interacts with other policies and actors — a systems-thinking addition.

Use it when
  • Evaluating a donor-funded development, aid, or humanitarian program against an internationally recognised standard
  • You need a common evaluation language across multiple funders or implementing partners
Caveat

OECD itself warns against mechanical "tick every box" application regardless of relevance. Increasingly criticised for Eurocentric origins and donor-centric framing — a live debate in the field about whose standards of "relevance" and "value" the criteria actually encode.

Evaluation Framework Patton, 2011
Developmental Evaluation
For when the program itself is still being invented. Evaluation embedded in real time as part of the innovation process, not bolted on at the end to judge a fixed design.
Core Premise

Most evaluation assumes a stable program design you're testing against fixed criteria. Developmental Evaluation assumes the opposite — the intervention is still emerging, adapting, and being co-designed with the evaluator feeding real-time data back into the design process itself.

Use it when
  • A genuinely novel intervention in a complex, unpredictable system (not a known model being rolled out)
  • The team needs to adapt the program design mid-stream based on what's being learned, not just measure a fixed thing
Caveat

Easy to misuse as an excuse for poor planning rather than genuine complexity — "developmental" should describe the problem's nature, not a justification for skipping rigour. Harder to satisfy traditional funder reporting requirements that expect a fixed logic model upfront.

Process Process Frameworks — the cycle of moving evidence into action
Process Framework Graham et al., 2006
Knowledge-to-Action (KTA) Framework
Models the full journey from raw research evidence to embedded practice — two linked components, not a single linear pipeline.
Two Components
Knowledge Creation (funnel) Action Cycle (7 stages)
What it's actually for

Knowledge Creation narrows raw inquiry → synthesis → user-friendly tools/products. The surrounding Action Cycle (identify problem → adapt knowledge to local context → assess barriers → select/tailor/implement interventions → monitor use → evaluate outcomes → sustain use) is explicitly iterative and non-sequential — you can enter or exit at any phase.

Use it when
  • You're starting from a clear, already-synthesised evidence base (e.g. a clinical guideline) and need a structured path to embedding it in practice
  • Adopted by Canadian Institutes of Health Research (CIHR) and widely used as the default knowledge-translation map
Caveat

Despite being highly cited, a systematic review found it's often cited as theoretical backing rather than genuinely operationalised step-by-step in practice — easy to reference, harder to actually run faithfully.
